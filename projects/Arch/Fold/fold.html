Folded Plates:
Structural Exploration

Fall 2015

Project Breif

This is a study of the use of folded plate technology as a long-span structure and as a beautiful alternative to conventional post-and-beam structures. Folded plate systems get their structural strength through the depth of the crease, similar to the depth of a truss. They can span very long distances by transferring loads along the surface which minimizes the number of ground anchors. The facets also have a playful character, thus integrating form and function (structure and aesthetics become inseparable). As enclosure is inherent to this type of structure, we are proposing a covered shelter over a bicycle rack on the campus of Virginia Tech. This research proposes a way of expressing folded plates in an experimental fashion and a joint that responds to custom angles and differently sized pieces that are inherent to a folded plate structure. The joinery method introduces a third element, a bent metal plate, similar to a biscuit joint where the wooden plates are slit, that is bent at an angle and inserted in the grooves and bolted in to keep it in place. This is also an investigation into the level of complexity attainable with the available technology (design software, robots, Computer Numerically Controlled machines), including the construction of tools specific to the needs of the project while trying to simplify and streamline the workflow. From initial sketching to the final construction phase, it is essential to integrate the computer as much as possible and as early as possible as an apparatus to quickly create and iterate ideas, refine them, and generate the construction algorithm to execute by the robots. The form-finding process is inspired by typical folded plate structures and precedent research. It is achieved using parametric modelling as a generative tool, structural analysis, and optimization software to refine the design for feasibility prospects.
